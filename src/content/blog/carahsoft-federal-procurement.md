Carahsoft generated $15.5 billion in revenue in 2024, ranking #26 on Forbes' list of America's largest private companies. They work with [300+ technology vendors](https://www.carahsoft.com/vendors) and [10,000+ resellers and systems integrators](https://www.carahsoft.com/partners/resellers-service-providers). They hold contract vehicles across NASA SEWP, NIH CIO-SP3, GSA MAS, ITES-SW2, and more. Since 2019, their publicly-reported federal contract revenue has doubled while the average among other distributors dropped 37%.

That's the scale. Now here's what they actually do.

## The Model

Carahsoft is a value-added reseller and distributor — they call themselves the "[Master Government Aggregator](https://www.carahsoft.com/buy)." The job is simple: hold the federal contract vehicles so commercial software vendors don't have to. Getting on a GSA schedule or a GWAC is slow, expensive, and procedurally brutal. Carahsoft has already done it. For companies like Salesforce, Palo Alto Networks, or Zoom, Carahsoft is the path of least resistance into a trillion-dollar market.

Vendor partners with Carahsoft, Carahsoft holds the paper, agencies buy through existing contract vehicles. Everyone wins — and Carahsoft clips a margin on every deal.

## The Investigation

In September 2024, the [FBI and Defense Criminal Investigative Service raided Carahsoft's Reston, Virginia headquarters](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/contracting/2024/09/fbi-dcis-raid-carahsoft-headquarters/). The DOJ has been investigating [since at least 2022](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2024/09/fbis-carahsoft-raid-comes-amid-allegations-price-fixing/399820/) — the allegation is that Carahsoft conspired with SAP, Accenture, and others to fix prices on sales to the U.S. military and federal agencies, a potential False Claims Act violation. DOJ lawyers complained that [for over a year, Carahsoft hadn't produced complete transaction records](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2024/09/fbis-carahsoft-raid-tied-years-price-fixing-allegations-other-vendors/399820/) for even a single project under investigation.

The case is ongoing. But it puts a hard question on the table: when one company controls this much of the federal IT distribution pipeline, the margin they earn isn't just a cost of doing business. It might be the product.

## The Real Problem

Carahsoft exists because federal procurement is too broken to navigate directly. The contracting complexity is so severe that it created an entire industry of specialists to manage it — and Carahsoft became that industry's dominant player. Every dollar they earn in margin is a dollar that reflects how much friction the government has built into its own buying process.

The model works. But the [investigation into price-fixing](https://fortune.com/2024/09/25/doj-investigates-sap-carahsoft-alleged-price-fixing/) shows what happens when a single intermediary gets big enough to stop competing and start coordinating.

Federal procurement reform isn't abstract. It's the difference between a healthy vendor marketplace and one company taking a cut of everything.
